# Variational Autoencoder

> [!abstract] One-line summary
> A generative model that learns a low-dimensional latent representation of data by jointly training a probabilistic encoder and decoder, optimized via a tractable lower bound on the data likelihood.

## The Problem It Solves

We want a model that can both **generate** new data (images, actions, etc.) and learn a meaningful **latent representation** $\mathbf{z}$. The natural approach is a latent-variable generative model:

$$p_\theta(\mathbf{x}) = \int p_\theta(\mathbf{x}|\mathbf{z}) \, p(\mathbf{z}) \, d\mathbf{z}$$

But this integral is intractable for complex decoders (e.g., neural networks) — we can't compute the likelihood $p_\theta(\mathbf{x})$ or the posterior $p_\theta(\mathbf{z}|\mathbf{x})$ exactly.

**Naive alternatives fail:**
- A standard autoencoder gives a latent space but no probabilistic structure — you can't sample from it to generate new data
- MCMC-based inference is too slow for large datasets
- Mean-field variational inference doesn't scale to the richness of neural network decoders

## How It Works (Step by Step)

### 1. The Generative Model

Assume data $\mathbf{x}$ is generated by:

$$\mathbf{z} \sim p(\mathbf{z}) = \mathcal{N}(\mathbf{0}, \mathbf{I}) \qquad \mathbf{x} \sim p_\theta(\mathbf{x}|\mathbf{z})$$

The **decoder** $p_\theta(\mathbf{x}|\mathbf{z})$ is a neural network parameterized by $\theta$ that maps latent codes to data.

### 2. The Intractable Posterior

To train, we'd like to maximize $\log p_\theta(\mathbf{x})$. But computing the true posterior $p_\theta(\mathbf{z}|\mathbf{x})$ requires the intractable integral above. VAE's key insight: **approximate the posterior with a learned encoder**.

### 3. The Encoder (Recognition Model)

Introduce an **encoder** $q_\phi(\mathbf{z}|\mathbf{x})$ — a neural network that maps data to an approximate posterior:

$$q_\phi(\mathbf{z}|\mathbf{x}) = \mathcal{N}(\boldsymbol{\mu}_\phi(\mathbf{x}),\; \text{diag}(\boldsymbol{\sigma}^2_\phi(\mathbf{x})))$$

The encoder outputs a mean $\boldsymbol{\mu}$ and variance $\boldsymbol{\sigma}^2$ for each input — not a single point, but a distribution.

### 4. The ELBO (Evidence Lower BOund)

Since we can't maximize $\log p_\theta(\mathbf{x})$ directly, we maximize a lower bound:

$$\log p_\theta(\mathbf{x}) \geq \underbrace{\mathbb{E}_{q_\phi(\mathbf{z}|\mathbf{x})}[\log p_\theta(\mathbf{x}|\mathbf{z})]}_{\text{reconstruction term}} - \underbrace{D_\text{KL}(q_\phi(\mathbf{z}|\mathbf{x}) \| p(\mathbf{z}))}_{\text{regularization term}}$$

This is the **ELBO**. Maximizing it simultaneously:
- Makes the decoder good at reconstructing $\mathbf{x}$ from $\mathbf{z}$ (reconstruction term)
- Keeps the encoder's approximate posterior close to the prior (KL term)

The gap between the ELBO and the true log-likelihood is exactly $D_\text{KL}(q_\phi(\mathbf{z}|\mathbf{x}) \| p_\theta(\mathbf{z}|\mathbf{x}))$ — as the encoder gets better at approximating the true posterior, the bound tightens.

### 5. The Reparameterization Trick

We need to backpropagate through the sampling operation $\mathbf{z} \sim q_\phi(\mathbf{z}|\mathbf{x})$, but sampling is non-differentiable. The trick: reparameterize the random variable:

$$\mathbf{z} = \boldsymbol{\mu}_\phi(\mathbf{x}) + \boldsymbol{\sigma}_\phi(\mathbf{x}) \odot \boldsymbol{\epsilon}, \qquad \boldsymbol{\epsilon} \sim \mathcal{N}(\mathbf{0}, \mathbf{I})$$

Now the randomness is in $\boldsymbol{\epsilon}$ (which doesn't depend on $\phi$), and gradients flow through $\boldsymbol{\mu}$ and $\boldsymbol{\sigma}$ normally.

### 6. Training Pipeline

```
Input x
   ↓
Encoder q_φ(z|x)  →  μ, σ
   ↓
Reparameterize: z = μ + σ ⊙ ε,  ε ~ N(0, I)
   ↓
Decoder p_θ(x|z)  →  x̂
   ↓
Loss = -ELBO = Reconstruction(x, x̂) + KL(q_φ(z|x) ‖ p(z))
```

For Gaussian $q_\phi$ and standard normal prior, the [[KL-Divergence]] term has a closed-form solution:

$$D_\text{KL} = -\frac{1}{2} \sum_{j=1}^{d} \left(1 + \log \sigma_j^2 - \mu_j^2 - \sigma_j^2 \right)$$

### 7. Generation (Test Time)

Sample $\mathbf{z} \sim \mathcal{N}(\mathbf{0}, \mathbf{I})$ and decode — the regularization during training ensures the latent space is smooth and meaningful near the prior.

## Why It Works

The ELBO creates a tension between two objectives:
- The **reconstruction term** pushes the encoder to preserve information in $\mathbf{z}$
- The **KL term** pushes the encoder to stay close to a simple prior

This tension forces the model to learn a *structured* latent space — similar inputs cluster together, and interpolating in latent space produces plausible outputs. The reparameterization trick makes the whole thing trainable end-to-end with standard SGD.

## Common Failure Modes

- **Posterior collapse**: When the decoder is too powerful (e.g., autoregressive), it ignores $\mathbf{z}$ entirely and the KL term goes to zero. The encoder just outputs the prior, and $\mathbf{z}$ carries no information. Mitigated by KL annealing ($\beta$-VAE) or weaker decoders.
- **Blurry outputs**: The Gaussian reconstruction loss encourages averaging over modes rather than committing to one, producing blurry samples (especially for images).

## Trade-offs

| | VAE | GAN | Diffusion |
|---|---|---|---|
| Training | Stable (ELBO) | Unstable (adversarial) | Stable but slow |
| Sample quality | Lower (blurry) | High (sharp) | Highest |
| Latent space | Structured, interpretable | Entangled | No explicit latent |
| Sampling speed | Single forward pass | Single forward pass | Many steps |
| Density estimation | Yes (ELBO) | No | Yes |
